Which Material Is the Best Thermal Insulator?

This website explains an experiment testing cotton, aluminium foil, bubble wrap, A4 printer paper, cotton cloth, and a no-insulation control to find which material slows the cooling of hot water most effectively.

View the science See the results

Written Abstract

This investigation tested how different insulating materials affect the rate of cooling of hot water. Identical 250 ml glass beakers were wrapped with cotton, aluminium foil, bubble wrap, A4 printer paper, and cotton cloth. A control beaker with no side insulation was also tested for comparison. Each beaker contained 200 ml of hot water at 70 ± 1°C. The beakers were kept in the same room, on the same bench, away from windows, doors, and fans, with the room temperature kept at about 22 ± 1°C.

Temperature was measured using a digital thermometer with 0.1°C resolution every 5 minutes for 20 minutes. Each material was tested three times. The results were analysed by comparing final temperature, temperature change (ΔT), and rate of cooling in °C/min. Bubble wrap was the most effective insulator because it had the lowest average cooling rate. This supports the scientific idea that materials which trap air reduce heat transfer by conduction and convection.

Project Snapshot

6 materials or conditions tested
3 repeat trials for reliability
20 minutes of cooling measured
0.25 °C/min average cooling rate for bubble wrap

Refined Research Question

How does the type of insulating material around an identical beaker affect the rate of cooling, in °C/min, of 200 ml of hot water over 20 minutes?

Overall Finding

Bubble wrap was the best thermal insulator in the test. It reduced heat loss because the air trapped inside its bubbles made it harder for heat energy to move from the hot water to the cooler surroundings.
