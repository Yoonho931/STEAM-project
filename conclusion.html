Discussion, conclusion and sources

Conclusion

The investigation showed that air-trapping materials were the most effective at reducing the rate of cooling.

Discussion: Impact of Science

This investigation shows how understanding heat transfer can be applied to real life. Bubble wrap and cotton were effective because they trapped air, which reduced heat transfer by conduction and convection. This principle is used in home insulation, warm clothing, food containers, and shipping materials. In buildings, insulation reduces energy use by slowing heat loss, which lowers heating costs and can reduce environmental impact.

The results also show that not all materials work in the same way. Aluminium foil can reflect thermal radiation, but it was not the best insulator in this test because it does not trap much air and can transfer heat by conduction. This is why insulation design often combines materials: a shiny reflective layer may be useful, but it is usually more effective when combined with air-trapping material.

Final Conclusion

The aim of this investigation was to determine which material was the best thermal insulator for a container of hot water. The results show that bubble wrap was the most effective material because it had the lowest temperature change, 5.0°C, and the slowest average cooling rate, 0.25°C/min. Cotton was the second most effective material, while the control with no side insulation cooled fastest.

The hypothesis was supported because the materials that trapped air, especially bubble wrap and cotton, reduced heat loss better than thin paper or no insulation. The investigation could be improved by using a data logger, testing more trials, measuring exact insulation thickness, and using a larger range of materials.

Reliability and Improvements

  • Repeat trials improved reliability by reducing the effect of random errors.
  • Using 70 ± 1°C starting water improved fairness.
  • Using identical lids reduced heat loss from the top equally.
  • Using a 0.1°C thermometer improved measurement precision.
  • A future test could use a data logger for more frequent readings.
