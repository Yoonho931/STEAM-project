Equipment, method and risk assessment

Testing Fairly and Safely

The method was designed to standardise starting temperature, water volume, beaker type, insulation coverage, room location, timing, and measurement precision.

Equipment Requested

Precise equipment list
Equipment Precise specification Purpose
Beakers or cups Six identical 250 ml heat-resistant glass beakers, same shape and thickness Hold 200 ml of hot water for each material and the control
Thermometer Digital thermometer with 0.1°C resolution, or school-safe alcohol/spirit thermometer; no mercury thermometer Measure water temperature accurately
Stopwatch Digital stopwatch or timer measuring to at least 1 second Record temperatures at equal time intervals
Measuring cylinder 200 ml measuring cylinder with 1 ml or 2 ml graduations Measure the same water volume each time
Insulating materials Cotton wool, aluminium foil, bubble wrap, A4 80 gsm printer paper, and cotton cloth squares Independent variable being tested
Fasteners Rubber bands or masking tape, same number for each beaker Hold insulation in place consistently
Extra control items Card lids with a thermometer hole, tray, paper towels, safety goggles Reduce top heat loss, contain spills, and improve safety

Method

  1. Place six identical 250 ml beakers on the same bench, in the same room and location, away from windows, doors, heaters, and fans.
  2. Cut each insulating material so it covers the sides and base of a beaker with the same surface area. Use one consistent layer or keep thickness as similar as possible.
  3. Label the beakers: control, cotton, aluminium foil, bubble wrap, A4 printer paper, and cotton cloth.
  4. Heat water to 70 ± 1°C. Use the same hot water source for all beakers in one trial.
  5. Measure 200 ml of hot water using the measuring cylinder and pour it into each beaker. Record the starting temperature within 30 seconds of pouring.
  6. Put the same type of card lid on every beaker, including the control, with a small hole for the thermometer. This reduces heat loss from the top equally.
  7. Record the water temperature at 5, 10, 15, and 20 minutes. Keep the thermometer in the centre of the water and avoid touching the side of the beaker.
  8. Repeat the investigation three times for each material.
  9. Calculate the average final temperature, temperature change (ΔT), and rate of cooling in °C/min.

Risk Assessment Based on CLEAPSS Student Safety Sheets

Hazards linked to method steps
Step Hazard Severity Control measure
Steps 4-5: heating and pouring water Hot water may cause scalds or burns. Medium Use teacher supervision, heat water to about 70°C rather than boiling, carry small volumes only, pour slowly on a stable bench, and wear safety goggles.
Step 5: using glass beakers Glassware may break and cause cuts. Medium Check glassware before use, keep beakers on a tray, do not use cracked glass, and tell the teacher immediately if breakage happens.
Step 7: measuring temperature Thermometer may break or be used incorrectly. Low to medium Use a digital or school-safe spirit thermometer, never a mercury thermometer, and keep the probe away from the glass sides and base.
Steps 2-3: preparing insulation Paper, cotton, cloth, and bubble wrap can clutter the bench and create slips or contact with hot water. Low Prepare materials before heating water, keep the bench clear, and keep all materials away from the kettle or heat source.
Steps 2 and 6: securing insulation Rubber bands may snap or tape may loosen near warm surfaces. Low Use bands carefully, do not overstretch them, and check the insulation is secure before hot water is added.
Step 9 and cleanup Water may still be warm when disposed of. Low Allow water to cool before disposal, pour slowly into the sink, and wipe spills immediately.

The risk assessment follows the safety approach used in CLEAPSS Student Safety Sheets: identify the hazard, link it to the activity, estimate risk, and apply control measures.

Testing and Redesign

After planning the first version, the method was improved by using repeat trials, measuring the starting temperature within 30 seconds, using identical card lids, and standardising the surface area of insulation. These changes reduce random error and make the data more reliable.
